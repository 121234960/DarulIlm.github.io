What are Bases?

Bases are fundamental chemical substances that are the chemical opposites of acids. They play crucial roles in maintaining pH balance in nature, industrial processes, and biological systems.

  • Lewis Definition: Substances that can donate a pair of electrons.

Key characteristics of bases include:

  • Bitter taste (though tasting them is dangerous!)
  • Slippery or soapy feel
  • Ability to neutralize acids
  • Conduct electricity in solution (electrolytes)
  • Turn red litmus paper blue

Did You Know?

The word "alkali" is often used interchangeably with "base," but technically refers specifically to bases that are soluble in water. The term comes from Arabic "al-qaly" meaning "the ashes," referring to early discoveries of alkaline substances in wood ashes.

Key Characteristics

  • Physical Properties: Often solid at room temperature (except ammonia), can be corrosive
  • pH Value: Have a pH greater than 7 (the higher the pH, the stronger the base)
  • Reactivity: React with acids to form salt and water (neutralization)
  • Conductivity: Strong bases are good electrolytes in solution
  • Indicator Tests:
    • Turn red litmus blue
    • Turn phenolphthalein pink
    • Turn methyl orange yellow
  • Corrosiveness: Strong bases can cause severe burns to skin and tissues

Try This at Home! (with adult supervision)

Experiment 1: Testing Household Bases

Materials: Red litmus paper, baking soda solution, soap solution, milk of magnesia, ammonia cleaner (diluted)

Procedure:

  1. Prepare weak solutions of each substance
  2. Dip separate pieces of red litmus paper into each solution
  3. Observe and record any color changes

Observation: The litmus paper should turn blue in basic solutions. Compare how quickly the color changes to estimate relative base strength.

Experiment 2: Neutralization Reaction

Materials: Baking soda, vinegar, clear glass, thermometer

Procedure:

  1. Place 1 tablespoon of baking soda in the glass
  2. Measure the temperature of the vinegar before adding
  3. Slowly add vinegar and observe the reaction
  4. Feel the glass to detect any temperature change

Observation: The mixture will fizz as carbon dioxide is produced. The reaction is endothermic (absorbs heat), so the container should feel cooler.
