What are Acids?

Acids are fundamental chemical substances that play crucial roles in both nature and industry. In aqueous solution, acids are characterized by:

  • A distinct sour taste (though tasting them is not recommended!)
  • The ability to turn blue litmus paper red
  • Reactivity with bases and certain metals to form salts
  • Conduction of electricity in solution
Scientific Definition: According to the Brønsted-Lowry theory, acids are proton (H⁺ ion) donors. The Arrhenius definition states that acids are substances that increase the concentration of H⁺ ions in aqueous solution.

Did You Know?

The word "acid" comes from the Latin word "acidus" meaning sour. This reflects one of their most noticeable properties that was observed since ancient times.

Key Characteristics

  • Taste: Sour (e.g., lemon juice, vinegar) - though tasting laboratory chemicals is dangerous!
  • Litmus Test: Turn blue litmus paper red
  • Reactivity: React with active metals (like zinc, magnesium) to produce hydrogen gas (H₂)
  • Neutralization: React with bases to form salt and water in a neutralization reaction
  • Conductivity: Act as electrolytes, conducting electricity in aqueous solution
  • pH Value: Have a pH value less than 7 (the lower the pH, the stronger the acid)
  • Corrosiveness: Many strong acids are corrosive to organic materials and metals

Strong vs. Weak Acids

The strength of an acid refers to its ability to donate protons (H⁺ ions) in aqueous solution.

Strong Acids: Completely ionize (dissociate) in water, meaning nearly all acid molecules break apart to release H⁺ ions. These make good electrolytes due to high ion concentration.

Examples of strong acids include:

  • Hydrochloric acid (HCl)
  • Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) - diprotic (donates two H⁺ ions)
  • Nitric acid (HNO₃)
Weak Acids: Only partially ionize in water, meaning only a fraction (typically less than 5%) of the acid molecules release H⁺ ions. They establish an equilibrium between ionized and unionized forms.

Examples of weak acids include:

  • Acetic acid (CH₃COOH) - found in vinegar
  • Carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) - in carbonated beverages
  • Phosphoric acid (H₃PO₄) - in cola drinks

Try This at Home! (with adult supervision)

Experiment 1: Acid-Base Reaction

Materials: Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate), vinegar, clear glass

Procedure:

  1. Place 1 tablespoon of baking soda in the glass
  2. Slowly add vinegar and observe the reaction

Observation: The mixture will fizz vigorously as carbon dioxide gas is produced. This demonstrates the neutralization reaction between an acid (acetic acid in vinegar) and a base (sodium bicarbonate).

Chemical Equation: CH₃COOH + NaHCO₃ → CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂↑

Experiment 2: Testing Acidity

Materials: Blue litmus paper, lemon juice, vinegar, milk, water

Procedure:

  1. Dip separate pieces of blue litmus paper into each liquid
  2. Observe any color changes

Observation: The lemon juice and vinegar will turn the litmus red, confirming their acidic nature. Milk may show slight acidity, while water should not change the litmus color.

Safety Note: Always wear protective goggles when conducting experiments, even with household chemicals. Never mix acids with other chemicals unless instructed.
